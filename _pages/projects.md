My research combines computational modelling, behavioural experiments, and neuroimaging to understand how people learn about emotional information and how these processes relate to mental health.


## Individual differences in emotion perception
People will differ in terms of how they display and interpret facial expressions of emotion. Here I investigated these individual differences in people's representations of different expressions, using 3D modelling and genetic algorithms. We developed a computerised task that allowed participants to visualise expressions they believed best represented different emotion categories, and allowed us to pinpoint these expressions within high-dimensional expression space. With this task, I showed that each of us carries a distinctive signature for representing facial expressions, and this remains stable across changes in face identity.

In another study I introduced a new framework for probabilistically modelling emotion inferences. Using the same task, I showed that by modelling each individual's 'perceptive fields' - regions within expression space that drive a response - we can predict how people will uniquely respond to expressions.

You can read about these studies in [Emotion](https://psycnet.apa.org/fulltext/2023-97693-001.html) and [Communications Psychology](https://www.nature.com/articles/s44271-024-00111-7).

## Negative affect and perceptual biases
Affective biases are central to mood and anxiety disorders, where individuals will often interpret ambiguous facial expressions more negatively. Using an adaptation paradigm and probing metacognitive judgements, we showed that neuropsychiatric measurements of negative affect influenced perceptual biases towards negative expressions. This highlights perceptual encoding as a potential mechanism underlying affective biases in mood and anxiety disorders.

You can read about this research in the [Journal of Psychopharmacology](https://journals.sagepub.com/doi/full/10.1177/02698811251409143)

## Acute stress effects on emotional learning under uncertainty
Forming expectations about the world around us is important for adaptive behaviour, and acute stress can affect how people learn when the world is uncertain. However, little is known about how stress can affect learned expectations within the social world. Participants completed an associative learning task in which emotional expressions were predicted by environmental cues. Crucially, we manipulated acute stress with the Threat of Scream paradigm. Preliminary results from computational modelling suggest that how we learn about the emotions of others may be disrupted in states of high stress.

## Pharmacological and 7T imaging interrogations of treatment mechanisms
The Locus-Coeruleus noradrenergic (LCNA) system has been theoretically linked to learning under uncertainty. Importantly, individual differences in how people estimate uncertainty may be associated with mental health difficulties and therefore provide insight into treatment mechanisms.

However few pharmacological studies have causally tested the involvement of the LCNA system in estimations of uncertainty and their relation to psychiatric symptoms.

In this large, multi-branch programme of work we combine ultra-high-field 7T neuroimaging, a pharmacological manipulation, behavioural and neuropsychiatric testing, physiological recordings, and hormonal measurements. In a double-blind placebo controlled branch we use atomoxetine to interrogate the role of noradrenaline in forming expectations about the world. Preliminary computational modelling shows atomoxetine reduces estimates of uncertainty for those who overestimate it, and increases estimates of uncertainty for those who underestimate it.

## The perception of facial expressions is influenced by our understanding of emotion concepts
Our perception of the world is guided by prior knowledge and expectations. However little is known about how this processing occurs in emotion perception. In this study I used representational similarity analysis with psychophysics to show that our perception of facial expressions is shaped by our higher-level understanding of emotion concepts (e.g. what it means to be happy). In a second study I showed that these concepts also influence how expressions are processed in the brain.

You can read about this research in [Cortex](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S0010945221002938)

## Interoception and emotion perception
Active inference models propose that the brain generate predictions about upcoming internal (interoceptive) and external (exteroceptive) signals, and research probing cardiac effects on emotion inferences support this hypothesis. However little is known about how we form these expectations to begin with. In this work, participants completed a task in which they implicitly learned to associate cardiac signals with emotional states of others. Results show that people can form these interoceptive-exteroceptive associations, and that the formation of these associations may be impaired for those high in neuropsychiatric traits.
