## Individual differences in emotion perception
People will differ in terms of how they display and interpret facial expressions of emotion. Here I investigated these individual differences in people's representations of different expressions, using 3D modelling and genetic algorithms. We developed a computerised task that allowed participants to visualise expressions they believed best represented different emotion categories, and allowed us to pinpoint these expressions within high-dimensional expression space. With this task, I showed that each of us carries a distinctive signature for representing facial expressions, and this remains stable across changes in face identity.

In another study I introduced a new framework for probabilistically modelling emotion inferences. Using the same task, I showed that by modelling each individual's 'perceptive fields' - regions within expression space that drive a response - we can predict how people will uniquely respond to expressions.

You can read about these studies in [Emotion](https://psycnet.apa.org/fulltext/2023-97693-001.html) and [Communications Psychology](https://www.nature.com/articles/s44271-024-00111-7).

## The perception of facial expressions is influenced by our understanding of emotion concepts
Our perception of the world is guided by prior knowledge and expectations. However little is known about how this processing occurs in emotion perception. In this study I used representational similarity analysis with psychophysics to show that our perception of facial expressions is shaped by our higher-level understanding of emotion concepts (e.g. what it means to be happy). In a second study I showed that these concepts also influence how expressions are processed in the brain.

You can read about this research in [Cortex](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S0010945221002938)

## Negative affect and perceptual biases
Affective biases are central to mood and anxiety disorders, where individuals will often interpret ambiguous facial expressions more negatively. Using an adaptation paradigm and probing metacognitive judgements, we showed that neuropsychiatric measurements of negative affect influenced perceptual biases towards negative expressions. This highlights perceptual encoding as a potential mechanism underlying affective biases in mood and anxiety disorders.

You can read about this research in the [Journal of Psychopharmacology](https://journals.sagepub.com/doi/full/10.1177/02698811251409143)
